Perfectionism

I wrote my first blog post on 22nd April. It took me more than two months to post this second one. In the First Blog post, I concluded that I would post every weekend and that didn't happened. In the beginning , I was trying to design the perfect website where I could show code, math equations, and table of index for the headings of the post, etc.

I thought so much about what to post for the second one. I had many ideas, but I didn't feel like I could complete those in a week. I thought of writing an article on how to center a div in CSS. But researching itself took me more than a week.

Then I wanted my website to have a code viewer and its output. So I started developing for that. I thought the UI should be responsive for mobile also. So the task took even more time. The task took too much time and I lost my motivation to complete it. I started looking for an easier topic, and I couldn't find a smaller topic to post. So I started procrastinating in the pretext that it needed too much work. Like that, the initial weeks are gone and my motivation was also gone and I shelved the project and moved on to a different one.

When I was reading an article from substack , it was describing exactly what I was experiencing. It mentions excellent points which I could relate to.

Here is one story from the article (the same story is in the book "Atomic Habits" also) that explains my situation very well :

In a photography classroom at the University of Florida, Jerry Uelsmann unknowingly designed the perfect experiment for understanding excellence. He divided his students into two groups.

The quantity group would be graded on volume: one hundred photos for an A, ninety photos for a B, eighty photos for a C, and so on.

The quality group only need to present one perfect photo.

At semester's end, all the best photos came from the quantity group.

The quantity group learned something that cannot be taught: that excellence emerges from intimacy with imperfection, that mastery is built through befriending failure, and that the path to creating one perfect thing runs directly through creating many imperfect things.

I had a perfect image of how my blog should be. I saw many cool blog designs, and I wanted all those features in my blog. But when I started working on it , I realized how much effort I needed to put to get that design and those features.

After my first post, I had many ideas for the blog post. But whenever I sat down to work on it , I couldn't finish the post because it didn't feel perfect to post it. So I didn't move forward with any of the ideas. After a month I moved to the next shiny new project.

This article fully resonated with me and I want to start posting every week even if it is not perfect.
